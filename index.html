What to Attend, What to Keep

Multi-Modal Representation Learning
for Contact-Rich Manipulation

Anonymous Author(s)

We present SkillFormer — a language-conditioned representation that decomposes manipulation into primitive skills and learns which modality to attend to and what states to keep at each phase, evaluated through skill progress estimation across contact-rich tasks requiring sensing beyond vision.

Paper Code Video Explore
Abstract

Robotic manipulation requires integrating diverse modalities — vision, touch, and language. Yet existing policies fuse modalities uniformly across all task phases, ignoring the phase-dependent temporal relevance of each modality.

We address this by decomposing long-horizon manipulation into primitive skills such as reaching, grasping, and alignment. Language serves as an interface that structures the sequence of primitives and guides sensor prioritization at each stage. Our model combines modality-specialized tokenizers, asymmetric temporal horizons for RGB and tactile, and a sparse event memory that retains skill-relevant states at their boundaries. We evaluate the learned representation through progress prediction — a structured diagnostic that directly reflects whether the representation captures the right modality at the right time — and compare against conventional multi-modal representations used in manipulation policies.

Dataset

Contact-Rich Manipulation Tasks

Each task requires different modalities at different phases. Completing the final skill primarily relies on tactile perception — twist completion, slip detection, and hidden cube identification cannot be inferred from vision alone.

Lamp Replacement

Replace a light bulb. The critical terminal skill — twist completion — must be detected from increased tactile force after the bulb is sufficiently rotated. Visual cues alone are ambiguous.

Bottle Capping

Cap a bottle by detecting slippage during cap sliding. Progress in the sliding stage is driven entirely by tactile feedback — slip signatures that register force transients invisible to RGB.

Blind Cube Search

Identify a hidden cube among pentagon and square shapes using tactile deformation patterns. The robot must distinguish contact geometry with zero visual information about the cube's location.

Architecture

How does it work?

Three design decisions address the core challenge of observation formation for contact-rich manipulation.

SkillFormer. CLIP ViT and FeelAnyForce encode RGB and tactile frames over modality-specific horizons. A frozen CLIP text model encodes the queried skill. Tokens carry temporal, modality, and event embeddings. A GPT-style transformer fuses them via bidirectional self-attention and decodes scalar progress.

Experiments

Progress Estimation as a Representation Probe

Progress estimation directly measures whether a representation captures the right modality at the right time. We compare SkillFormer against observation formulations common in manipulation policies. Strongest gains appear on skills whose completion depends on contact state, tactile evolution, or transitions between nearby manipulation phases.

✦

Tactile matters at boundaries. On twist bulb and slide cap, SkillFormer achieves near-zero MAE. Baselines without tactile-specialized encoding fail precisely where contact dynamics signal completion.

✦

Event memory resolves ambiguity. The w/o-Events model shows large errors on align cap (0.09 vs 0.01), confirming that skill boundaries are ambiguous without a stored reference to the terminal state of the previous skill.

✦

Blind cube search is fully tactile-dominant. SkillFormer substantially reduces delay and false positives on inspect box compared to FiLM, which cannot distinguish cube geometry from tactile deformation alone.
